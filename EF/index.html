ELECTRIC FIELD

Before we begin looking into how transistors work, we first need to understand the electric field. In this chapter, we see how two charged surfaces interact with each other.

Electric Field

Any positive or negative charge generates an electric field in the space surrounding it (typically represented by arrows). Let's assume we have an infinitely large surface (where the size of the plane in the y + z directions are much larger than in the x direction).

Click on the surface to the right to charge it.

The amount of charges on the surface is for representation purposes. In reality, there are many more charges by orders of magnitude.

Charge Density

Here, we have an array of positive charges. For the side of the sheet facing left, the direction of the electric field (on the x plane) is negative. For the side of the sheet facing right, the direction of the electric field is positive.

Try changing the charge density and see how it affects the electric field vector.



We can see that the strength of the electric field depends on the amount of charge on the surface.

Two Surfaces

Letâ€™s look at what happens when we put two sheets of opposite charge together. We can see that the left and right sides of the electric fields cancel each other except for the area in between in the two sheets. This effect is called screening. In the area in between the two sheets, the electric field due to positive and negative charges add to double the strength of the net electric field.

You can click and drag to move the negatively charged sheet... Use the buttons below to show the screening effect or toggle the arrows.

These two surfaces that generate an electric field is the basis of a capacitor.




Imperfect Screening

The charge density in the two arrays may not always match exactly. In that case, the electric field due to the positive and negative charges are not fully cancelled. If we have a negatively charged surface with less charge density, the net electric field in the area between in the two sheets is no longer double, but still stronger. Use the buttons below to show the screening effect or toggle the arrows.






More Sheets

Let's try adding another sheet.

We can see that the second negative sheet creates another in-between area where the electric field amount is less than the first in-between area. Both of the negative sheets' electric fields in the -x direction work together to cancel out all of the positive sheet's electric field in the +x direction.

You can hover over each sheet to see its charges more clearly.






Multiple Sheets

We can also use multiple sheets, with charges of varying (strengths). Then the sheets will screen out the positive electric field over a distance.

Use the slider to flow the higher charge density amount left and right.






Volumes

When we use multiple negative sheets, the negative charge is then distributed over a distance where eventually - total electric field to due positive charge is equal to total electric field due to negative charge. This set of negative sheets can be considered a volume. The volume might be a sheet of metal that is a semiconductor.

The graph for a volume can be represented as a slope.

Use the slider to change the width of the volume.
