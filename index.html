ELECTRIC FIELD

Before we begin looking into how transistors work, we first need to understand the electric field. In this chapter, we see how two charged surfaces interact with each other.

[1] 3D Field

Any positive or negative charge creates an electric field in the space surrounding it (typically represented by arrows). Let's assume we have an infinitely large surface (where the size of the plane in the y + z directions are much larger than in the x direction).

Click on the right graphic to charge this surface with electrons.

[2] Change Electrons

If we have an array of positive charges, for the side of the sheet facing left, the direction of the electric field (on x plane) is negative. For the side of the sheet facing right, the direction of the electric field is positive. Use the slider to change the amount of charge and see how it affects the electric field vector.

The strength of the electric field depends on the amount of charge on the surface.

Try changing the amount of charge.

[3] Change Charge

What about an array of negative charges? What directions for the electric field would the left and right sides of the sheet be?

[4]

For the side of the sheet facing left, the direction of the electric field is positive. For the side of the sheet facing right, the direction of the electric field is negative. Letâ€™s look at what happens when we put two sheets of opposite charges together. We can see that the left and right sides of the charges cancel each other except the middle section. This effect is called screening.

That leaves us with an intersecting portion, where the positive and negative charges add to double the strength of the electric field.

Drag your mouse to move the negatively charged sheet. Use the buttons below to show the screening effect or hide arrows.





[5] Imperfect Screening

In screening, the strength of the electric charges in the two arrays may not always match exactly. In that case, the charges are not fully cancelled. If we have a negatively charged surface with less charge, the charge of the intersecting portion is no longer double, but still stronger.





[6] More Sheets

Let's try adding another sheet. What do you notice?

We can see that the second negative surface creates another portion where the electric field amount is less than the first portion. Both of the negative surfaces' electric fields in the -x direction work together to cancel out all of the positive surface's electric field in the x direction.








[7] Multiple Sheets

What if we try using multiple sheets? We can put multiple sheets, with charges of varying (strengths). Then the sheets will screen out the positive electric field over a distance.









[8] Slope

The negative charge is then distributed over a distance where eventually, total positive charge = total negative charge. The set of negative sheets can be considered a volume. The volume might be a sheet of metal that is a semiconductor.
